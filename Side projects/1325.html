Detailed Process Flow with Critical Parameters

1

Degreasing & Cleaning

Alkaline cleaning at 60-80°C for 2-3 minutes to remove oils and organic contaminants. Critical for surface preparation.

2

Pickling (Acid Treatment)

Hydrochloric acid (15-20%) or sulfuric acid (5-10%) at 40-60°C for 3-5 minutes to remove mill scale and rust.

3

Rinsing & Drying

Multiple rinses with demineralized water (conductivity < 50 μS/cm) followed by hot air drying at 120-150°C.

4

Flux Application

Zinc ammonium chloride flux (ZnCl₂·3NH₄Cl) applied to prevent oxidation before entering furnace.

Coating Formation Mechanism

Stage 1: Initial Reaction

Upon immersion, aluminum reacts with iron to form FeAl₃ (θ-phase) at the interface. This occurs within 1-2 seconds at 700°C.

Reaction: Fe + 3Al → FeAl₃

Stage 2: Diffusion Layer Growth

Fe diffuses through FeAl₃ layer to form Fe₂Al₅ (η-phase) at the steel interface. Growth rate: 1-2 μm/s at 700°C.

Diffusion-controlled growth

Stage 3: Silicon Effect

In Al-Si baths, silicon reduces Fe₂Al₅ growth and promotes FeAl₃ formation, resulting in thinner, more ductile layers.

Si content: 9-11% in Type 1 coatings

Mechanism: H₂ dissociates at steel surface (600-700°C) → H atoms diffuse into steel matrix → Causes hydrogen-induced cracking.

Critical Safety Warning

Hydrogen absorption increases exponentially above 400°C. For high-strength steels (≥800 MPa), furnace temperature should be limited to 600°C maximum with hydrogen levels below 300 ppm. Post-coating baking at 200°C for 24 hours can help diffuse out trapped hydrogen.

Atmosphere Control Best Practices

Continuous Monitoring: Install redundant O₂ and H₂O analyzers with automatic alarms at 15 ppm O₂ and -25°C dew point.
Pressure Control: Maintain 3-8 mbar positive pressure relative to outside atmosphere to prevent air ingress.
Entry/Exit Seals: Use labyrinth seals with nitrogen curtains at strip entry/exit points.
Gas Purity: Use industrial grade nitrogen (99.995% min) with on-site purification if needed.

Quality Assurance Measures

Leak Testing: Quarterly pressure decay tests with helium mass spectrometry for leaks < 10⁻⁶ mbar·L/s.
Sampling: Daily gas samples analyzed by gas chromatography for precise composition verification.
Microstructure Analysis: Weekly cross-section analysis to monitor intermetallic layer thickness and structure.
Documentation: Complete traceability with batch records of all process parameters.

Hydrogen Embrittlement Mechanism

Process: Hydrogen atoms diffuse into steel at high temperature → Collect at grain boundaries and dislocations → Reduce material ductility → Cause delayed cracking under stress.

Prevention: Control furnace atmosphere (low H₂), limit temperature (<600°C for high-strength steels), post-coating baking (200°C for 24h), use modified Al-Mg-Si baths that reduce hydrogen absorption by 40-60%.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about hot-dip aluminizing

Why is hydrogen control critical in the furnace atmosphere?

Hydrogen atoms can diffuse into steel at high temperatures, causing hydrogen embrittlement. This reduces ductility and can lead to delayed cracking under stress. For high-strength steels (>550 MPa), hydrogen levels must be kept below 300 ppm, and for critical applications, below 100 ppm.

What happens if oxygen levels are too high in the furnace?

Oxygen reacts with the steel surface to form iron oxides (FeO, Fe₂O₃) before aluminizing. This oxide layer prevents proper wetting and bonding of aluminum to steel, resulting in bare spots, poor adhesion, and coating discontinuities. Oxygen must be kept below 10 ppm, ideally below 5 ppm.

How does silicon in the aluminum bath affect coating formation?

Silicon (9-11% in Type 1 coatings) reduces the growth of the brittle Fe₂Al₅ phase and promotes the formation of FeAl₃. This results in thinner, more ductile intermetallic layers with better adhesion and formability. Silicon also lowers the melting point of the aluminum bath.

What is the optimal cooling rate after dipping?

Cooling rate significantly affects microstructure. For thin gauges (<0.050"), rapid quenching (40-60°C/s) produces fine grains with excellent adhesion. For thick gauges (>0.090"), slower cooling (15-25°C/s) prevents cracking. Controlled cooling is essential to optimize coating properties.

How are furnace atmosphere parameters monitored and controlled?

Modern systems use zirconia oxygen sensors (1 ppm resolution), electrolytic hygrometers (dew point measurement), and gas chromatography for hydrogen analysis. Redundant sensors with automatic alarms and control loops maintain optimal conditions. Positive pressure (3-8 mbar) prevents air ingress.
